# the lighthouse keeper of the sonoran

she'd been given the lighthouse and told to keep the light burning. no one mentioned the desert.

she assumed it was a mistake at first. filed the paperwork. waited for reassignment. meanwhile she climbed the stairs each evening and lit the lamp because — well. it was there. the lamp was there and the dark was there and some part of her found the argument complete.

the ships never came. obviously. but other things did.

on the forty-third night, something with the shape of a question mark moved at the edge of the beam. she watched it graze the light and veer away. on the fifty-first night, two somethings, same shape, moving in slow parallel. by the third month she'd given up trying to describe their outlines. they didn't want to be described. they wanted the light.

she started keeping records of different things: what she'd been thinking when they came, whether it mattered.

*thinking about the letter i never sent. three somethings, large.*

*thinking about nothing. seven. close enough to see that they don't have faces so much as the suggestion that faces could have been arranged there differently.*

*thinking about bread. one. very small. seemed embarrassed.*

she tried to hail them once. climbed down to the desert floor, walked toward the nearest one. it stopped moving but didn't leave. she got close enough to feel something — not heat, not cold. more like the sensation of standing next to a word you've been trying to remember.

she stood there for a long time. it stood there too. eventually she went back inside and made dinner and the light kept turning.

she wrote her supervisor again. described what she'd seen. the supervisor wrote back: *the light should be operational by 2200 hours each evening. report any equipment failures.* she filed this response in the drawer where she kept the other ones.

the somethings, she'd started thinking, were not ships. they were also not not-ships. they were something for which ship was an adjacent word — the way a photograph of a door is adjacent to going somewhere.

at the end of the year she got a transfer notice. new keeper coming in three days. she was needed elsewhere.

she packed her notebooks. stood at the lamp for a long time on the last night, her hand on the housing, watching them move below in the beam.

she thought: someone should warn the next person.

she left a note on the kitchen table. it said: *they don't want anything from you specifically. they just want the light to be on when they come. that's all. i thought that would feel small. it doesn't.*

she didn't explain what "they" meant. the next keeper would figure it out, or they wouldn't, and either way the lamp would go on burning and the somethings would go on coming and the desert would go on being a desert with a lighthouse in it, which was,

she thought,

enough.
