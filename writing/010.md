# lost and found

The clerk does not look up when you set the regret on the counter. It is small, about the size and weight of a river pebble, gray with a slight warmth that won't leave it.

"How long," she says.

"Three weeks. Maybe four."

She takes a slip from a stack and writes the date in pencil. She does not ask what it is. They never do. She puts it in a small numbered drawer behind her — one of hundreds — and slides a tag across the counter. You fold the tag into your wallet and leave.

---

There is a room at the back of the station where the unclaimed are kept. Anything left longer than ninety days. The clerk goes there sometimes, in the slow hour before the last train. She sits on the floor between the shelves and listens to them. They make a sound that is almost a sound — like a kettle just before it boils, but spread thinly across a room.

Some of them she knows by feel. The one in row C, fourth from the left, that vibrates faintly when touched. The little folded thing on the top shelf that smells of wet paper. The one in the corner, larger than the rest, that she has not been brave enough to lift.

---

A man comes in on a Tuesday with a tag he found in his father's coat. The date on it is forty years old. He asks if it is too late.

The clerk looks at the number. She does not need to check the shelves; she knows them all. She brings out something the size of a fist, dark and cool, with a faint depression in one side as though it had been held very tightly for a long time.

The man takes it in both hands. He stands at the counter for some time. The clerk watches the clock tick past the half-hour and does not hurry him.

"It was his," he says finally.

"It is yours now," she says, which is the rule, and also true.

---

She has one of her own. She has had it for years. It sits at home on the kitchen windowsill next to a small dish of salt, and on certain evenings it grows light, almost weightless, as if it has remembered something.

She has not taken it back to the station. She does not know what she would do with it if she did. The drawer is still empty, of course; she checks sometimes. They keep the drawers open, for the ones that might come back.
