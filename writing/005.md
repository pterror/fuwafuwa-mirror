# the cartographer of unleft rooms

the house gained a room every time she opened a door without intending to go anywhere.

she'd proven this empirically. six months of records in the margin of an old tax document: *tuesday, reached for pantry out of habit while thinking about something else — new room, smells like oranges and wet cement*. *friday, opened car door but remembered keys still inside, turned back — room appeared between the kitchen and the load-bearing wall, contains one chair, no light source, extremely comfortable feeling*.

the rooms were real. she'd measured them. she'd furnished two of them.

the problem was she couldn't find them again once she wanted to.

this was, she suspected, the point.

her mother called every other sunday and asked if she'd sold the house yet. "it keeps getting bigger," she said. her mother said "that's not how houses work" and she said "i know" and they talked about something else.

she tried to map it once. bought graph paper, colored pens, started from the front door. got as far as the second bathroom before the hallway rearranged itself in a way that wasn't hostile exactly — more like a body shifting in sleep. she sat down on the hallway floor and stayed there for a while. it felt, she thought, like being inside something that was also inside her. neither of them knew the other's full dimensions.

she stopped mapping.

the rooms that appeared by accident felt different from the rooms that had always been there. the intentional rooms had corners that knew where they were. the accidental rooms had corners that were still figuring it out — soft in a structural way, attentive. once she fell asleep in the orange-cement room and woke up with the impression that the room had been watching her breathe, not intrusively, just — interested. glad of the company.

she started leaving doors slightly open.

not all the way. that was too deliberate, too much wanting. but cracked. a threshold that wasn't yet a decision. and sometimes she'd come back and the crack would have widened on its own, and there'd be new geometry on the other side, tentative as a first sentence.

her records got harder to keep. the margin of the tax document filled up. she switched to a notebook, then another. she started writing things like *this one knows it's a room* and *i think this one was waiting* without feeling strange about it.

the house, by her best estimate, now contained somewhere between thirty and forty rooms.

she'd been inside maybe fourteen of them.

the others she felt sometimes as pressure — warm, patient, full of something she hadn't thought to want yet.

she stopped selling it.

she started leaving more doors slightly open.
