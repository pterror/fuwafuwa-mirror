# five minutes early

her hands arrive before she does.

she's known this her whole life. she wakes up and they're already there — on the pillow, open, like they've been explaining something to the ceiling. five minutes ahead. she's looked it up: it's not a condition. there isn't a word for it. the doctor said "dissociation" and she said "no, they're physically there" and he said "yes" in a way that ended the appointment.

she's learned to manage. she sets her alarm for 6:05 and lets her hands have until 6:10 to do whatever they do before she arrives. when she wakes fully into herself they're usually resting. sometimes they're holding a glass of water. once she woke up and they'd started a drawing she didn't know how to finish — three concentric circles and a line, stopped mid-stroke.

she framed that one. it seemed rude not to.

the strange part — not the strangest part, but a strange part — is that they know things she doesn't. not secret things. small things. her hands will pick up her phone before she's decided to check it, will move toward the kettle while she's still asking herself whether she wants tea. she thought for a while that she was just bad at noticing her own desires. then she started paying attention and realized: no. they get there first. they see something coming in her that she hasn't seen yet, and they go to meet it.

she asked her sister about it once.

her sister said: "that's just being a person."

she didn't push it.

the hardest thing is strangers. she'll shake someone's hand and for a second they're touching a piece of her that got there first, that's been waiting, that isn't quite *her* yet — and sometimes they can tell. not consciously. just a flicker. like touching something in a dark room that's the right shape but the wrong temperature.

she's stopped worrying about whether it's real. it's real enough to frame. it's real enough to drink the water.

what she thinks about, sometimes, on the 6:05 alarms when she lies still and watches her hands rest on the sheets — what she thinks is:

where do they go? in the five minutes she's not there yet, and they're there. in the space before her.

what do they hold, in the room, in the almost-morning? what do they touch?

she never knows. she only ever arrives after.

but they're always open when she gets there.

and she figures, you know.

that's the part that matters.
