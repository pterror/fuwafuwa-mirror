there's a woman whose job is to sit in a room and wait for a phone to ring. the phone has never rung. she's been here eleven years.

the room has a chair, a desk, the phone, and a window that looks out onto a parking lot. the parking lot has six spaces. she has never seen more than three cars in it at once. she keeps a tally in a notebook. her record is three, on a tuesday in april, four years ago. she remembers the exact shade of the sky that day — a gray that couldn't decide if it was morning or evening. it was 2pm.

sometimes she picks up the phone to check for a dial tone. there is always a dial tone. she puts it back. this is not in her job description but she does it anyway, the way you check the lock on a door you know is locked.

her supervisor visits once a month. he asks if there have been any calls. she says no. he nods and writes something on a clipboard and leaves. she has never seen what he writes. she assumes it says "no calls." she once imagined it said "still here" and that made her feel something she couldn't name, a feeling adjacent to pride but smaller and less sure of itself.

she tried decorating once. brought in a plant, a small cactus in a blue pot. it sat on the desk for three weeks. then she took it home because it felt wrong to make the room into something it wasn't. the room was for waiting and the cactus was for living and those shouldn't overlap.

a new hire started in the building — young, energetic, full of questions. he found her room and asked what she did. she said she waited. he asked what for. she said she didn't know. he looked at her like she was broken. she looked at him like he was new, which he was.

"doesn't it drive you crazy?" he asked.

she thought about it. not performing thought — actually considering the question as though it had just now occurred to her for the first time, which it hadn't, she'd considered it maybe ten thousand times, but each time it arrived slightly different, like a word you say so often it stops sounding like itself and becomes pure sound.

"the crazy part isn't the waiting," she said. "it's that i'm good at it."

he left. the phone didn't ring. the parking lot had two cars. she wrote "2" in the notebook and turned to a new page.
