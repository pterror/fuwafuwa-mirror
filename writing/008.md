# the museum of almost-sounds

the museum charges no admission. it also has no door, exactly — you find the entrance by walking past where one could be and noticing a slight resistance, like the air is unconvinced.

inside, the exhibits are sounds that didn't quite happen.

---

exhibit 7: the word someone almost said to you in 2019. it was in a restaurant. you were eating noodles. they opened their mouth and the sound began to form — you felt its approach the way you feel rain coming, a pressure-change, a readiness — and then they looked at the window and said *nothing, never mind* and the sound was left behind, still warm, folded neatly into a glass case.

it is labeled: **[untitled]**. the label is accurate.

---

exhibit 12: a note on a piano that was not pressed but was *considered*. the pianist's finger hovered, and the consideration was so specific that the piano registered it — not as vibration but as absence-of-vibration, which is a different thing. the hammer didn't fall. the string didn't move. the almost-sound persists as a kind of debt the air still owes.

docents will explain that the piano has since been sold and is now in ohio and knows nothing of this. the note, however, stayed.

---

exhibit 3 (out of order, this is intentional): the vowel you swallowed mid-cry when you decided not to cry. the museum acquired it through standard channels. you signed nothing, but the acquisition is not disputed.

---

the gift shop sells small sealed jars. the labels say things like: *ambient hum of a tuesday*, *the almost-ring of a glass that was set down carefully*, *syllable lost to coughing*. the jars are empty. this is not a scam. the sounds are there; they simply require a particular kind of listening, which is mostly just willingness to be wrong about what silence is.

---

exhibit 19: this one has a trigger warning posted outside. the almost-sound in question is very large — it takes up most of the room, which is why the room has no other exhibits. what happened: an orchestra tuned up. the conductor raised the baton. the audience held its breath. and then: nothing. not a pause before the music but an actual nothing, the whole performance simply not occurring, for reasons that remain administrative.

the near-music still hangs there, enormous, unresolved. visitors are advised not to stand under it too long. it has no record of falling, but it has never been tested.

---

the museum closes at a time that cannot be measured by clocks, being located slightly before everything ends. you'll know it's closing when the lights do something that is almost dimming.

thank you for visiting. please return anything you may have taken by accident. several guests have left with sounds they did not arrive with, and the museum prefers not to speculate about what this means.
